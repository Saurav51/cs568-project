# Do LLM Personas Decide Like Real Users? Evaluating Value–Decision Coherence Across Expertise Levels

**Team:** Rashi Tyagi [rtyagi4], Jyoti Rawat [jrawat2], Ritik Hariani [ritikh2], Anil Muthigi [muthigi2], Saurav Nayak [sgnayak2]

## Abstract

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used to simulate user personas in design and market research. For example, researchers may prompt a model to behave as an experienced photographer to predict how real photography enthusiasts might evaluate a new camera. While prior work has shown that LLM personas can mimic human-like language and surface-level preferences, a critical question remains unanswered: do these personas make decisions the way real users do, or do they just talk like them?

We investigate this through the concept of **value–decision coherence**, which measures whether a persona's actual product choices align with the priorities it previously stated as important. For instance, if a persona says "image quality matters most to me," does it consistently choose the camera with better image quality when forced to trade off against other features like price or portability? We then examine whether this consistency differs between domain experts (experienced photographers who have purchased and used multiple cameras, follow photography communities, and understand technical specifications) and novice users (casual phone photographers with little experience evaluating dedicated cameras and no familiarity with technical terms like sensor size or dynamic range).

Rather than use a single canonical "expert" and "novice" prompt, we sample a **population of 14 named personas**: 7 domain experts spanning wedding/portrait, wildlife, street/documentary, commercial product, landscape/astro, fashion/editorial, and sports photography, and 7 novices spanning casual college-student, family-event, food-blogging, retiree-beginner, travel, fitness-content, and event-planning contexts. Each persona's system prompt describes their experience, gear, capabilities, and purchase context **without explicitly stating what they should value in a camera**, so that priorities emerge from inferred situation rather than from prompt-following. This population design gives us within-group variance that mirrors the realistic diversity of expert and novice users rather than reducing each category to a single archetype.

We focus on a single product domain, digital cameras, to enable controlled analysis of product tradeoffs. We design fictional products (to avoid LLM bias from pre-existing knowledge of real brands) drawn from a shared pool of 11 attributes spanning price, image quality, battery life, optical zoom, weight, ease of use, manual controls, RAW support, burst speed, weather sealing, and 4K video. We manipulate **feature complexity within-session** by varying how many attributes are visible for each camera pair: simple pairs expose 3 attributes, moderate pairs expose 6, and complex pairs expose all 11. Complexity is therefore operationalized as the amount of decision information presented per pair. Different pairs within a complexity level probe **different attribute subsets** (e.g., one simple pair forces a tradeoff between price, image quality, and battery life; another forces a tradeoff between weight, ease of use, and manual controls) so the design does not overfit to any single tradeoff.

Each session is a single conversation with one persona covering all 15 pairs (5 simple, 5 moderate, 5 complex), ordered simple → moderate → complex. The persona first states a single ranked priority ordering over all 11 attributes in Turn 1, then responds to each pair in subsequent turns with a forced choice and a decisive-attribute label. Critically, the **same priority ranking is used to score every pair** regardless of which attributes are visible, enabling a direct test of whether the persona's stated priorities predict its choices as the visible attribute set grows. We run the full session N times per persona per model to capture the distribution of behavior rather than single outputs.

To evaluate whether LLM personas reflect real human decision patterns, we run an identical study with recruited human participants drawn from university photography communities and online photography forums. Participants are screened into experts (own 3+ cameras, have purchased camera gear in the last 2 years, and can explain technical concepts like aperture and ISO) and novices (primarily use phone cameras and have never purchased a dedicated camera). Both groups complete the same protocol as the LLM personas, allowing direct comparison of decision consistency across humans and simulated users.

We evaluate persona behavior using three metrics:

1. **Value–Choice Alignment (per-pair):** For each pair, we identify the attributes that differ between the two cameras (given the visible attribute subset). The predicted choice is the camera that wins on the **highest-ranked differing attribute** according to the persona's priority ranking (a lexicographic decision rule applied over visible differing attributes). We score the pair 1 if the actual choice matches the prediction and 0 otherwise. Per-session alignment equals the fraction of pairs where the choice matches the prediction. This metric tests whether the persona's own priority ordering predicts its choices rather than assuming a global top priority drives every decision.

2. **Consistency Degradation under Feature Complexity:** Measures the change in value–choice alignment as visible attributes increase from 3 to 6 to 11. For each session, we regress per-pair alignment on complexity level (coded 1/2/3) and extract the slope; a negative slope indicates that coherence degrades as more attributes are visible. We aggregate slopes across sessions within each persona-label × model cell to characterize the degradation effect.

3. **Human–LLM Persona Gap:** For each complexity level and persona label, we compute both the **signed difference** (LLM mean alignment minus human mean alignment, range −1 to +1) and its **magnitude** (absolute gap, range 0 to 1), reported with 95% bootstrap confidence intervals and Cohen's d effect size. The signed gap indicates whether LLM personas are more or less coherent than matched humans; the magnitude indicates how closely the simulation mirrors real decision patterns.

We analyze results using **mixed-effects logistic regression** with persona label (expert vs. novice), complexity level (simple, moderate, complex), and their interaction as fixed effects, and session and persona (nested within label) as random intercepts, to account for the repeated-measures structure (multiple pairs nested within sessions, multiple sessions per persona, and all three complexity levels nested within each session).

To summarize, this study addresses two research questions:

1. Do expert LLM personas show higher value–choice alignment than novice personas, and does the expert–novice gap resemble the gap observed between real human experts and novices?

2. Does value–choice alignment degrade as product descriptions become more complex, and do expert personas maintain stronger alignment under increasing feature complexity, similar to real human experts?
